Curse of the Corsair

Technical Skills Used: Unity, C#

General Skills Used: Enemy Design, Multidisciplinary Communication, Agile Workflow

Curse of the Corsair was my first time working as a game designer with WolverineSoft. I was assigned to be the game's enemy designer, so naturally I worked closely with the player designer to develop a satisfying combat loop. Then I wrote design documentation for the three regular enemies, two level bosses, and the final boss that I had designed. The documentation is very thorough, it includes attacks(complete with timing, diagrams, and damage values), behavior trees, use cases, and a written explanation of what strategy a player might employ to defeat the enemy. In addition to iterating on those designs, I utilized my Unity experience to bring implementation in line with my designs and fix bugs. My prior experience as a programmer on Subtension also allowed me to communicate my needs effectively with the programming team, which kept my squad running like a well-oiled machine!

I'm very proud of my design for the final boss. I was told that I'd need to design a final boss for the game about a week after I had turned in my design for the boss of the second level, and by that point we knew that the art department wouldn't be able to make many more new assets. I thought for a while about how to make a climactic and intimidating final boss without needing a whole bunch of new art. This was a really difficult problem, but I really like the solution I came up with! And I think that the behavior of the boss makes it super aggressive and fun to fight, even for me, and I had to fight it a lot of times to tune the timing and damage values. You can see my documentation for the enemies in Curse of the Corsair below!

Curse of the Corsair is a 2D action-adventure game made as a case study of Hyper Light Drifter by Heart Machine. It was developed in Unity and C# during the 2023 winter semester by WolverineSoft Studio: the student-led game development organization at the University of Michigan. I worked with a team of around ~35 other designers, artists, programmers, producers, marketers, and sound designers throughout this project. We utilized the Agile design process, along with tools such as Jira, Confluence, and Bitbucket to streamline our development process.

Soul of the Forest

Technical Skills Used: Unity, C#

General Skills Used: Project Management, Scoping, Enemy Design, Multidisciplinary Communication, Agile Workflow

The development of Soul of the Forest was filled with many unique challenges. It was our first time attempting an RPG and a narrative-focused game. In addition to doing all of the enemy design for the game, as design lead I had to navigate the complex relationship between narrative and game design. Because we were doing so many things with this game that we had never attempted before, such as cutscenes, branching dialogue, and turn-based combat, the scope had ballooned well beyond what was possible for us to accomplish in a single semester. This led us to add another semster to the dev time, so we could put out a polished game that we were all proud of.

Unfortunately, the second semester of development on Soul of the Forest came with its own challenges. I was promoted to project lead during this time, which meant that I had to manage both my game design team and the leads of the other departments. We also discovered during our playtests that people didn't think the combat system that I designed was very fun. So over a week, I had to redesign the combat system to focus on the more fun aspects of my initial design, without forcing our programmers to rewrite massive portions of our codebase. What I came up with tested much better during our playtests, and it allowed each battle to feel more unique and exciting!

Since scope was such a large issue in our first semester, we naturally had to scale it back during the second. Part of my job as project lead was to check in with the other department leads and ensure that everything was still in scope. We managed to reduce the scope enough in that second semester that the most important parts of the game were able to make it in with a very high level of polish. During the debugging and polishing phase of development I got in the engine to fix things like enemy behavior, mini-games, damage balancing, and the tutorials and UI.

Soul of the Forest is a 2D RPG game based on modern RPGs such as Undertale and Franken. It was developed in Unity and C# during the 2023 fall and 2024 winter semesters by WolverineSoft Studio: the student-led game development organization at the University of Michigan. I worked with a team of around ~60 other designers, artists, programmers, producers, marketers, and sound designers throughout this project. We utilized the Agile design process, along with tools such as Jira, Confluence, and Bitbucket to streamline our development process.

Broadcast Day

Technical Skills Used: Unity, C#, Jira

The game was initially inspired by other level-based 3D platformers such as Sonic Adventure and Toree 3D. As development progressed we switched to a more exploration-focused level design that directed the player towards talking to the many NPCs that populate the levels.

Class Game Engine

Technical Skills Used: C++, Lua, Make, Box2D, LuaBridge, GLM, SDL

General Skills Used: Project Management

This is a game engine that I made over the course of a single semester in Winter 2024 as part of EECS 498: Game Engine Architecture at the University of Michigan. Its architecture is heavily based off of Unity, and it is written in C++. The scripting language is Lua, I used Luabridge to connect the Lua scripts written by developers to the C++ core. I linked the Box2D and SDL libraries to this engine to be used for physics and rendering respectively. This engine runs on Mac, Windows, and Linux.

I also wrote my own custom particle system for the engine, it's also based off of Unity's particle system and has a huge number of options to choose from so developers can get the exact effect they envisioned in their heads!

Wolverine Engine

Technical Skills Used: C++, Lua, CMake, Box2D, Sol, Data-Oriented Design, Testing/Debugging

General Skills Used: Project Management, Scoping

Wolverine Engine is a custom 2D game engine I wrote with 2 of my peers during the summer of 2024. The engine is heavily based on Unity and its method of composition for game development. I wrote the engine in C++ using common libraries such as SDL2 for rendering and Box2D for physics.

One of my goals during development was to create a seamless build environment that works across multiple platforms so that developers could build for OSX and Windows without too much trouble. To meet this goal I learned how to use CMake and link all of the external libraries that Wolverine Engine uses. Now building for OSX or Windows is as simple as running 2 commands in your terminal!

A game engine needs to store large amounts of the same class constantly within its storage (actors, components, scenes, etc...), so one of my professors suggested that we code portions of it in a data-oriented way. Using data-oriented design in our game engine means that in large games there will be far fewer cache misses so the engine should be much smoother. After a few weeks of learning how to code that way, I was able to create much more efficient actor and component systems for the engine.

Wolverine Engine was a project that presented me with challenges I had never had to face as a programmer before. Creating a build system that works across multiple platforms, thinking about code in an all-new data-oriented way, and connecting Lua to C++ using Sol were only some of the tasks I faced. Creating a game engine meant I had to prepare for a near-infinite number of unique use cases, so optimization and complex test suites were more important than ever.
